Topic 2: Epicureans

Epicureanism is a philosophy that focused on how to be a happy person, especially in the face of challenges, though its understanding of what happiness is differs from a lot of common, current ideas about happiness. Epicureanism became so popular that it became a dominant religion in the Mediterranean world, especially during the Roman empire. We will read the original Epicurean, Epicurus, and then the most prominent and influential Epicurean author, Lucretius, whose work De Rerum Natura is widely described as a classic of world literature.

Reading

  1. Epicurus, “The Principal Doctrines,” in The Epicurus Reader pages 32–36.
  2. Epicurus, “Letter to Herodotus,” in The Epicurus Reader pages 5 ff (you can skip or skim sections 58–62, 67–74, which seem to me less important).
    • Think about: What are Epicurus’s basic beliefs about reality and how we know about it? Are they similar to or different to ours? Do they have any implications for how (he thinks) we should approach life?
  3. Epicurus, “Letter to Menoeceus,” in The Epicurus Reader pages 28–31.
  4. Lucretius, from On the Nature of Things (De Rerum Natura), pages TBA
